Oberlin Food Hub

The Oberlin Food Hub is a nonprofit founded in 2017 to build an efficient, equitable regional food system. It supports sustainable agriculture and small- and mid‑size producers while ensuring consistent access to healthy, affordable food for communities. The hub offers a centralized ordering platform and manages logistics—aggregation, cold storage, invoicing and customer service—so farmers can focus on growing food. It purchases products from area farms and supplies them to food banks, independent grocers, colleges, restaurants and farm stores.

Equity Focus

By pooling supply from many small farms and handling logistics centrally, the Food Hub creates fair market access for growers who might otherwise struggle to reach wholesale buyers. The organization emphasizes fairness, transparency and shared prosperity; it plans to expand services with a shared‑use kitchen incubator to process Grade B produce and build climate resilience.

Rosby Companies

Rosby Companies operate an agricultural, horticultural and recycling business on a scenic 70‑acre site in Brooklyn Heights near Cleveland. They produce their own soils, mulches, organic humus and compost, and run a resource recycling program that reclaims construction and demolition debris from the waste stream. The operation integrates farming with environmental stewardship by turning waste into inputs for local growers.

Waste‑to‑Value Model

The company’s composting facility returns organic matter to the soil while diverting material from landfills. By producing mulches and humus locally, Rosby Companies reduce the need for imported soil amendments and support circular nutrient cycles within the regional food system.

Stutzman Farms

Stutzman Farms in Millersburg, Ohio, produces certified organic flours, oats and other grains using an on‑site mill. The family‑run business supplies organic granolas and flours to local markets and partners with about 20 certified organic farmers—many of them Amish with 10‑ to 15‑acre fields—to grow grains. Grain is milled with old‑fashioned equipment, and sprouted grains and gluten‑free options are available.

Partnerships with Amish Farmers

Stutzman Farms sells most of its products through Amish bulk food stores across several states. By providing seed and a market for small producers, the company helps sustain a network of independent farms. The farm’s puffer machine, used to make puffed cereals, illustrates how small innovations can add value to local grain crops.

Cleveland’s Greenhouse Industry (Historical)

From the late 19th century through the mid‑20th century, Greater Cleveland became the “Greenhouse Capital of America.” Martin Ruetenik built the first greenhouse in Brooklyn Heights in 1885, and by the mid‑1920s about 50 businesses maintained 80 acres of greenhouses, growing tomatoes, lettuce and cucumbers. The industry expanded to 400 acres under glass by the early 1960s, employing roughly 1,000 hothouse farmers—many of them Puerto Rican migrants. Produce was sold through roadside stands, markets and the Northern Ohio Food Terminal, enabling restaurants and households to enjoy fresh vegetables year‑round.

Rise and Decline

Greenhouse farming was laborious and expensive, requiring misting systems, boiler‑heated steam pipes and careful pollination. Cheap coal initially made heating affordable, but rising energy costs and pollution in the 1960s and 1970s, coupled with competition from Canadian subsidized greenhouse producers, led to a rapid decline. Many greenhouses closed or switched to flowers and ornamental plants. The R.O. Apelt Sons operation, once a 3.5‑acre hothouse producing cucumbers, dismantled its greenhouse in 2016 after switching to hydroponic cucumbers in 2000.

International Specialty Produce (ISP)

International Specialty Produce, Inc. is a Cleveland‑based wholesale distributor that imports hydroponic, organic and conventional produce from around the world. Founded in 1994, the company sources tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers, eggplant and numerous other fruits and vegetables. Over time it expanded to include ISP Services, Cleveland Tofu and ISP Logistics.

Warehousing & Logistics

  • Warehouse near the Cleveland Food Terminal with refrigerated, frozen and dry storage.
  • Cross‑docking, re‑palletizing, boxing, labelling, shrink‑wrapping and federal inspection services.
  • Freight brokerage with on‑call staff available 24/7/365, using 53‑foot refrigerated trailers and experienced drivers.
  • Helps customers minimize border crossing delays, detention pay and fuel costs while eliminating the need for their own trucks.

Although ISP’s model is not specifically an equity program, its logistics capabilities provide a backbone for moving fresh produce into and out of Northeast Ohio, enabling small retailers and restaurants to access a wide variety of foods.

ACEnet & Nelsonville Food Hub

ACEnet (Appalachian Center for Economic Networks) operates the Athens Food Ventures Center—one of the first shared‑use commercial kitchens in the U.S.—and the Nelsonville Food Hub and Business Incubator. The Ventures Center houses a central kitchen, dry packaging room, thermal processing room and warehouse. Approximately 65 food entrepreneurs per year use the facility, which holds multiple licenses for food service, cold storage and frozen food operations.

As a business incubator, ACEnet provides affordable production space, cooler and freezer storage, and assistance with product development and licensing. Through the Appalachian Accessible Food Network (a partnership with Rural Action and Community Food Initiatives), ACEnet helps accelerate distribution of local foods in Appalachian Ohio, strengthening access, production and consumption while reducing barriers for startups.

Northern Ohio Food Terminal & Sanson Company

The Northern Ohio Food Terminal opened in June 1929 on a 34‑acre site and once housed 100 merchants, employing about 1,500 people and handling roughly $140 million worth of food annually. During the late 1950s and early 1960s the terminal distributed nearly $200 million in produce each year, making it a vital organizing hub for regional agriculture. Produce arrived via railcars and trucks and moved quickly to wholesalers and retailers.

Today’s Role

While most of the original merchants have closed, Sanson Company—founded in 1914—remains the anchor wholesaler. Sanson provides retail, wholesale and food‑service customers with fresh produce backed by safety and quality programs. The company distributes local, organic and specialty produce to grocery stores and warehouses, supports local growers by purchasing their products, and operates a 25,000‑square‑foot certified organic facility.

Rust Belt Riders

Rust Belt Riders is a Cleveland‑based service that collects organic waste from restaurants and redistributes it to local farms and gardens as fertilizer. The company began with co‑founders Daniel Brown and Michael Robinson riding a bicycle with a trailer to pick up food scraps from restaurants. By turning waste into compost, they close the loop between urban eateries and regional farms.

Scaling Up Through Policy & Partnership

Composting at scale required a Class II composting permit and a suitable site. Rust Belt Riders worked with the Ohio EPA to expand the exemption for community compost piles from 300 to 500 square feet, enabling more gardens to compost legally. They later partnered with Kurtz Brothers to secure the Cloverleaf Composting Facility, allowing them to produce more compost in one month than they previously could in a year. Their potting mixes have been ranked among the best in research trials, and they were named Best Small‑Scale Composter in the U.S.

Green Field Farms Cooperative

Green Field Farms is a cooperative founded in 2003 by Amish and Conservative Mennonite farmers and businessmen to revitalize family farms through sustainable agriculture. The cooperative emphasizes culture, sustainability and community, aiming to foster economic stability for member families while supplying fresh, nutritious food under a trustworthy brand.

Natural Fertilizer & Soil Health

The cooperative launched a natural fertilizer division that offers soil testing and custom‑blended fertilizers tailored to individual farm needs. These blends help over 1,000 farmers improve soil health and balance nutrient levels, emphasizing long‑term generational benefits. The program underscores the cooperative’s commitment to regenerative practices and provides an equitable alternative to conventional chemical inputs.

Parker Bosley – Cleveland’s Local Food Pioneer

Parker Bosley, often called the “grandfather of Cleveland’s local food,” grew up on a dairy farm and studied cooking in Paris before opening Parker’s New American Bistro. Over two decades he built a menu that relied exclusively on foods produced by Ohio and nearby Pennsylvania farmers. His menu changed daily to reflect what was in season, and he visited farms to encourage specific varieties and breeds.

Fair Prices & Farmer Relationships

Bosley cultivated a network of cheese producers and small farms, paying farmers a premium (e.g., $10 per pound for artisan cheese) to ensure profits flowed directly back to them. He championed small‑scale, sustainable farming and challenged the notion that cheap food should come at the expense of natural resources and rural economies. His advocacy laid the groundwork for Cleveland’s farm‑to‑table movement.

Shagbark Seed & Mill

Shagbark Seed & Mill offers single‑source, Ohio‑grown certified organic heirloom grains and dry beans. Since 2010, the mill has connected family farms to grocers, restaurants, schools and health centers, and sells to food access sites at rock‑bottom pricing. Shagbark advocates for a “Staple Foods Revolution,” arguing that large seed and chemical corporations have reduced flavor, nutrition and climate resilience in staple crops.

Community & History

Founders Michelle Ajamian and Brandon Jaeger tested high‑nutrition bean and grain varieties with a USDA grant in 2007 and launched the Appalachian Staple Foods Collaborative in 2009. Community volunteers, lenders and friends helped them build the first certified organic mill in Ohio in 2010. Shagbark emphasizes that local grain systems require collaboration between farmers, millers, bakers and consumers.

Harbor Gardens

Located in Ashtabula County, Harbor Gardens is a local food market and demonstration kitchen designed to build community around self‑reliance and food security. The general store offers products sustainably grown and made in the county and hosts more than 50 local vendors. Shoppers find seasonal produce, grains, beans, cheese, sourdough bread, maple syrup, handmade items and more.

Classes & Knowledge Sharing

Beyond retail, Harbor Gardens provides tours and classes on cooking, canning and fermentation, helping residents learn practical skills for preserving and preparing local food. This educational component underscores equity by empowering community members to participate in food production and preservation.

Fresh Fork Market

Fresh Fork Market is a weekly subscription service connecting more than 100 family farms within 75 miles of Cleveland to consumers. Members choose from omnivore, carnivore, vegetarian or vegan bags and pick up a weekly box of groceries at over 20 locations. Each box contains seasonal produce, pasture‑raised meats, whole grains, eggs, dairy and value‑added items.

  • Weekly subscription fosters consistent income for small farms.
  • Customization allows families to select the right size and dietary style.
  • Online shop offers additional local items such as honey, jam and artisanal products.

Over 15 years, Fresh Fork has built relationships with farmers and refined its distribution to serve the community, effectively functioning as a modern CSA with flexible pickup points.

By sourcing exclusively from nearby farmers and producers, the shop shortens the supply chain and ensures fair pay to growers. The model demonstrates how small urban retailers can sustain local agriculture through direct purchasing and transparent pricing.

The Farmer’s Rail

The Farmer’s Rail grew out of Brunty Farms, where Jeff and Melanie Brunty began raising pasture‑raised animals nearly 20 years ago to offer neighbors meat they could trust. As their farm expanded, they realized the need to control processing and opened a farmer‑owned butcher shop in 2018. Today the Farmer’s Rail includes restaurants with from‑scratch kitchens and continues to supply Brunty Farms’ meats alongside products from other local vendors.

This vertical integration—from pasture to butcher to table—ensures transparency and fair compensation to farmers while providing consumers with high‑quality, locally sourced meals. The business model also builds community around food, celebrating the connection between farmers and diners.

Food Terminals & Produce Auctions (Historic)

In the early 20th century, food terminals were the primary organizing facet of local agriculture. Cleveland once hosted seven or eight terminals, relying on local farms to supply these bustling hubs. Produce trains from the West Coast—called “fruit blocks”—traveled east with perishable goods that often had priority over passenger trains. The Pennsylvania Railroad Fruit Auction & Sales Building in Pittsburgh’s Strip District, built around 1926, stretched 1,500 feet and spanned four city blocks. Fruit and produce were unloaded from railcars and auctioned in the early morning so goods reached markets and restaurants by opening time.

These terminals thrived thanks to the proximity of rail lines, but as refrigerated trucking became widespread, rail‑based produce distribution declined. Many terminals were demolished or repurposed; only a handful of operations, like the Northern Ohio Food Terminal (now Sanson Company) and the Consumers Produce facility in Pittsburgh, remain today. The history underscores how infrastructure shaped regional food economies and how transportation innovations can shift the balance between local and industrial supply chains.
